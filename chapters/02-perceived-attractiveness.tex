\chapter{Perceived Attractiveness}

\section{The Perceived Attractiveness Function}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{perceived attractiveness function} is a mapping
\[
  a : O \times S \times T \times C \to [0,1]
\]
where $O$ is the set of observers, $S$ is the set of perceptible stimuli, $T$ is the set of time points, and $C$ is the set of contexts.
The value $a(o, x, t, c)$ represents the degree of attractiveness that observer $o \in O$ assigns to stimulus $x \in S$ at time $t \in T$ under context $c \in C$, with $0$ denoting complete indifference and $1$ denoting maximal desire.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{Stimuli}

The set $S$ consists of any perceptible entity or feature capable of eliciting an attraction response.
This includes whole individuals, physical features (e.g., hair, body type), sensory traits (e.g., voice), behaviors or gestures, and presentation choices (e.g., clothing, style), as well as combinations thereof.
This formulation deliberately avoids restricting attractiveness to whole persons, allowing partial or abstract stimuli to be evaluated.

\subsection*{Context}

Context is understood here as everything external to the agents themselves.
The observer and the stimulus are the agents; time is the dimension along which the interaction unfolds; context is the world they are embedded in.
This is a philosophically clean separation: the function $a$ keeps its agents and its dimension distinct from the situational conditions that modulate how those agents relate.

Each context $c \in C$ is modeled as an element of a structured product space.
Rather than fixing a single decomposition, we treat $C$ as an open product type:
\[
  C = C_1 \times C_2 \times \cdots \times C_n
\]
admitting whatever decomposition the theory demands without altering the signature of $a$.
This gives us a stable core function that can accommodate new contextual dimensions in later chapters without redefinition.

As a concrete illustration, one natural decomposition is:
\[
  c = (c_0,\, \alpha,\, \nu,\, \dots)
\]
where $c_0$ denotes the situational setting (venue, occasion), $\alpha$ the aesthetic or appearance conditions, and $\nu$ the social visibility --- whether others are watching, and in what capacity.

\begin{remark}
We state the following as a standing convention throughout this book: \emph{$C$ is an open product space, admitting decomposition $C = C_1 \times C_2 \times \cdots \times C_n$ as the theory requires, without altering the signature of $a$.}
This means any contextual factor introduced in a later chapter is implicitly a new coordinate of $C$, not a modification to the attractiveness function itself.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Conventional Attractiveness}

The function $a(o, x, t, c)$ is inherently subjective: different observers may assign different values to the same stimulus under identical conditions.
A notion of \emph{conventional attractiveness} can be derived by aggregating over a population of observers:
\[
  \bar{a}(x, t, c) = \mathbb{E}_{o \in O}\bigl[a(o, x, t, c)\bigr].
\]
